Tupig

A traditional Pangasinan delicacy made from glutinous rice, coconut, and sugar, wrapped in banana leaves and grilled over charcoal until smoky and sweet.

  • Where to try it: Roadside stalls along Pangasinan highways
  • Best known for: Grilled sticky rice wrapped in banana leaves
  • Flavor: Sweet, smoky, and slightly chewy
  • Best paired with: Coffee or hot chocolate

What is Tupig?

Tupig is a native Filipino delicacy popular in Pangasinan and nearby provinces. It is made from a mixture of glutinous rice flour, coconut milk, sugar, and young coconut strips, wrapped in banana leaves and grilled over hot charcoal.

The banana leaves give it a distinct smoky aroma while keeping the rice cake moist and soft inside.

How Tupig is Made

The preparation of tupig is a traditional process often done in roadside stalls or family kitchens. The mixture is spread thinly onto banana leaves, folded, and placed directly on charcoal grills.

As it cooks, the banana leaves are continuously turned to prevent burning while allowing the natural sugars to caramelize slightly.

Cultural Importance

Tupig is commonly served during holidays, fiestas, and family gatherings. It is also a popular “pasalubong” (souvenir food) for travelers passing through Pangasinan.

For many locals, the smell of freshly grilled tupig along highways is a nostalgic reminder of home and provincial life.

Where to Find the Best Tupig

You will often find the best tupig being sold in roadside stalls in towns like Lingayen, Bayambang, and along major highways in Pangasinan.

Vendors usually grill them fresh on-site, so visitors can enjoy them warm and freshly cooked.

Why Tourists Love It

Travelers enjoy tupig because it is simple yet unique. The combination of smoky banana leaves and sweet coconut rice creates a flavor that is both comforting and memorable.

Travel Tip

Buy tupig fresh off the grill for the best experience. It tastes best when warm, especially when paired with coffee during early morning or evening travel stops.
