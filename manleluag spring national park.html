Manleluag Spring National Park

Manleluag Spring National Park, officially designated as the Manleluag Spring Protected Landscape, is a protected natural area in Barangay Malabobo, Mangatarem, Pangasinan, Philippines. It is known for its geothermal hot springs, diverse wildlife, and lush forest ecosystems within the Zambales Mountain Range.

Key Facts

  • Location:Mangatarem, Pangasinan, Philippines
  • Area:1,935.17 ha (core); 965.09 ha buffer
  • Established:1934 (forest reserve); 1940 (national park); 2004 (protected landscape)
  • Governing body:Department of Environment and Natural Resources
  • IUCN category:V – Protected Landscape/Seascape

Geography and Natural Features

Manleluag Spring National Park is a protected landscape centered around two ophiolitic hot springs near the slopes of the extinct Mount Malabobo, rising about 281 m above sea level. These springs produce highly alkaline waters that emerge from the serpentinizing rocks of the Zambales ophiolite, creating natural hot‑spring pools amid a lush tropical forest. Traversing the park, streams like the Baracbac and Basican rivers feed a network of forested pools, creeks, and shaded pathways that invite visitors to explore this verdant mountain environment.

The terrain varies from gently rolling hills to moderately steep forest slopes, with an average elevation of around 315 m. Tropical vegetation blankets much of the park, with tall trees and undergrowth supporting a rich ecosystem. The overall setting is a refreshing contrast to coastal and lowland landscapes, offering a retreat into cooler, greener highland scenery where hot springs and dense forests converge.

History and Legal Status

Manleluag was first established as a forest reserve in 1934 under Proclamation No. 659, intended to protect its unique springs and surrounding forested land. Later, in 1940, it became a national park through Proclamation No. 612 signed by President Manuel L. Quezon, further recognizing its importance as a natural attraction.

In 2004, the area was reclassified as a protected landscape under Proclamation No. 576, signed by President Gloria Macapagal Arroyo in accordance with the National Integrated Protected Areas System (NIPAS) Act, expanding both its protection and ecological management. This designation helps ensure that its hot springs, forest habitats, and associated wildlife receive ongoing conservation and sustainable use.

Today, Manleluag Spring National Park is managed with policies aimed at balancing recreation and conservation — preserving its natural and geological heritage while allowing visitors to experience its unique features responsibly.

Biodiversity

The park supports a rich array of wildlife and plant life thanks to its variety of habitats. Manleluag is home to more than 90 bird species, including iconic forest dwellers like the Philippine frogmouth, rufous hornbill, and flame‑breasted fruit dove, making it a rewarding destination for birdwatchers and nature lovers. Its forest also supports mammals such as the Philippine deer and warty pig, adding to the ecological richness of the protected area.

Additionally, research has found that the hot springs themselves host specialized microbial communities adapted to the extreme alkalinity, offering scientists insight into unique biological systems and deep‑subsurface ecosystems. These aspects contribute not only to the park’s biodiversity but also to its scientific significance.

Recreation and Tourism

Accessible via the Pangasinan–Tarlac Road, Manleluag Spring National Park offers a refreshing escape into nature with a variety of outdoor activities. Visitors can bathe in the natural hot springs pools, hike shaded forest trails, and explore picnic areas ideal for family outings or group gatherings. A viewing tower within the park provides scenic vistas of Pangasinan’s green landscape, while ranger‑led trails let visitors immerse in nature.

The park’s trails range from gentle strolls to more adventurous treks, appealing to hikers and nature enthusiasts alike. With designated picnic spaces and hot‑spring pools of varying temperatures, visitors can relax and enjoy the health‑benefits of geothermal bathing amid a tranquil forest setting.
